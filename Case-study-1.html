01 — The Problem

Customers Were Overwhelmed and Abandoning Orders

The Urban Plate had recently launched their mobile ordering app across all 15 locations, but the data told a concerning story. Despite strong initial downloads (12K+ in the first month), order completion rates were sitting at just 42%, well below the industry benchmark of 70%.

Customer feedback revealed a pattern: users felt overwhelmed by the sheer number of choices presented all at once. With 120+ menu items spanning breakfast, lunch, dinner, drinks, and desserts, customers were experiencing classic decision paralysis.

"I spent 10 minutes scrolling through the menu and still couldn't decide what I wanted. I just gave up and ordered from somewhere else." — User Interview Participant #7

02 — Research & Discovery

Understanding the Decision-Making Process

I led a comprehensive research initiative to understand why users were struggling with menu navigation. We combined quantitative analytics with qualitative user research to get a complete picture.

Research Methods

  • 15 in-person user interviews with regular customers
  • Analytics review of 5,000+ user sessions
  • Competitive analysis of 8 restaurant apps
  • A/B testing of 3 navigation patterns
  • Eye-tracking studies with 10 participants

Key Research Questions

  • How do users naturally categorize menu items in their minds?
  • What triggers decision fatigue during menu browsing?
  • Which information hierarchy reduces cognitive load?
  • How can we guide users without restricting choice?
  • What role does visual design play in decision-making?

Insight 1: Contextual Thinking

Users mentally categorized items by meal occasion (breakfast, lunch, dinner) before considering food type. Our menu prioritized food categories (salads, sandwiches, etc.) which didn't match their mental model.

Insight 2: Paradox of Choice

Presenting all 120 items simultaneously created analysis paralysis. Users who saw filtered subsets (20-30 items) made decisions 3x faster.

Insight 3: Visual Overload

Every item had an image, creating visual clutter. Users spent more time processing images than reading descriptions, leading to slower decisions.

Insight 4: Missing Guidance

First-time users wanted recommendations or popular items highlighted. Without social proof or curation, they felt lost in the options.

03 — The Solution

A Smarter Menu Architecture Built on Behavioral Science

Based on our research insights, I designed a new menu experience that reduced cognitive load through progressive disclosure, smart categorization, and strategic visual hierarchy. The solution balanced freedom of choice with guided discovery.

01

Context-First Navigation

Users first select their meal occasion (breakfast, lunch, dinner), immediately reducing visible items by 60-70%.

02

Smart Categorization

Within each meal, items grouped by preparation time, dietary preferences, and popularity rather than traditional food categories.

03

Curated Collections

Featured sections like "Staff Favorites," "Quick Bites," and "Customer Top 10" provide decision shortcuts for uncertain users.

04

Progressive Visual Reveal

Item images shown only on detail view, keeping the browse experience clean while still providing visual confirmation before ordering.

Before: Old Menu Structure

  • 120 items displayed simultaneously
  • Generic categories (Salads, Sandwiches, etc.)
  • All items had equal visual weight
  • No personalization or recommendations
  • Infinite scroll with no clear sections
  • Images for every single item

After: New Menu Structure

  • 20-35 contextually relevant items per view
  • Occasion-based navigation (Breakfast, Lunch, etc.)
  • Featured collections prominently displayed
  • Smart recommendations based on preferences
  • Clear sectioning with quick-jump navigation
  • Strategic imagery only where needed
04 — Key Design Decisions

Balancing Business Goals with User Needs

Every design decision involved careful consideration of both user experience and business objectives. Here's how I navigated key trade-offs throughout the project.

Decision 1: Limiting Initial Choices

Challenge: Business stakeholders worried that hiding items would reduce discoverability of the full menu.

Solution: I demonstrated through A/B testing that users exposed to 30 items had 3x higher order completion than those seeing 120 items, even though they explored fewer total items. Revenue per completed order was actually higher in the limited view.

Result: Stakeholders approved the contextual filtering approach.

Decision 2: Image Strategy

Challenge: Marketing team invested heavily in professional food photography and wanted every item showcased.

Solution: I proposed using images strategically in featured sections and detail views while keeping the browse experience text-focused. Eye-tracking data showed users spent 70% more time processing images than reading descriptions.

Result: Faster browsing without sacrificing visual appeal.

Decision 3: Personalization Level

Challenge: How much personalization without being creepy or limiting serendipitous discovery?

Solution: Implemented lightweight personalization: "Based on your previous orders" section limited to 4 items, with a clear "View All" option. No algorithmic hiding of items.

Result: 35% of repeat customers used personalized recommendations without feeling restricted.

Decision 4: Dietary Filters

Challenge: Users with dietary restrictions needed easy filtering but most users didn't want extra UI complexity.

Solution: Made dietary badges subtle but visible on all items, with optional filter toggle in navigation. Filters persisted across sessions for users who activated them.

Result: 15% of users activated filters, giving them a tailored experience without cluttering the default view.

05 — Implementation & Testing

Rolling Out Changes with Data-Driven Validation

Rather than a big-bang launch, I advocated for a phased rollout that allowed us to validate assumptions and iterate based on real user behavior. This approach reduced risk while building stakeholder confidence.

01

Prototype Testing

Interactive prototypes tested with 25 users across 3 locations. Gathered feedback on navigation patterns and information hierarchy.

02

Beta Launch

Released to 10% of users (1,200 people) in 2 pilot locations. Monitored metrics daily and conducted 5 user interviews per week.

03

Iteration Phase

Made 12 refinements based on beta feedback, including adjusting featured section prominence and refining dietary filter UX.

04

Full Rollout

Gradually expanded to 100% of users over 4 weeks, monitoring key metrics at each stage to ensure consistent improvements.

07 — Key Learnings

What I'd Do Differently Next Time

Every project teaches valuable lessons. Here are the key insights I gained from this experience that I'll carry forward to future work.

The most important lesson: Start with understanding how users naturally think about the problem space, not how your business categorizes it. Our breakthrough came when we stopped organizing by food types and started organizing by meal occasions.

What Worked Well

  • Grounding design decisions in behavioral science research
  • Phased rollout that allowed for iteration
  • Strong partnership with data analyst for metric tracking
  • Regular stakeholder updates with quantitative results
  • Balancing user needs with business constraints

What I'd Improve

  • Start user research earlier in the ideation phase
  • Build more sophisticated personalization algorithms
  • Test with more diverse user segments initially
  • Document decision rationale more thoroughly
  • Create better handoff documentation for engineers
